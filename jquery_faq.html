• Yosemite National Park

    While Yosemite is famous for its stunning granite cliffs and waterfalls, it's also home to a grove of ancient giant sequoia trees known as the Mariposa Grove. One of the trees in this grove, named the "Grizzly Giant," is estimated to be between 1,900 and 2,400 years old, making it one of the oldest living trees on Earth. These giant sequoias are among the largest trees by volume in the world and are a remarkable natural wonder within the park.

Visitors to the park can often see across the river to Mexico, and it's a unique aspect of the park's landscape and cultural history.

  • Yellowstone National Park

    Yellowstone National Park is home to the oldest, and one of the most picturesque, superintendent's houses of any national park. This historic building, known as the "Superintendent's Residence," is located at Mammoth Hot Springs and was constructed in 1884. It serves as the residence for the park's superintendent and is an iconic part of the park's history and architecture.

  • Zion National Park

    One of the most famous and unique hikes in Zion National Park is "The Subway." This hike takes you through a stunning slot canyon with narrow passages and turquoise pools. What's interesting is that you need a permit to hike "The Subway," and the permit is so sought after that it's distributed through a lottery system due to its popularity. The unique geology and beauty of "The Subway" make it a must-see for many visitors to the park.

  • Rocky Mountain National Park

    Rocky Mountain National Park boasts Trail Ridge Road, which is the highest continuously paved road in the United States. The road reaches an elevation of 12,183 feet (3,713 meters) above sea level. Traveling along this scenic route provides breathtaking panoramic views of the Rocky Mountains, including alpine tundra landscapes, wildlife, and an opportunity to experience the dramatic changes in elevation within the park. Trail Ridge Road is a popular attraction for visitors seeking stunning mountain vistas.

  • Grand Teton National Park

    Grand Teton National Park is known for its rugged mountain scenery, and many of the park's iconic features were shaped by glaciers during the last ice age. The Grand Tetons themselves are jagged peaks carved by glacial activity. The U-shaped valleys and pristine alpine lakes found throughout the park are also evidence of the profound glacial influence on the landscape. This glacial history has left a lasting mark on the park's stunning natural beauty.

  • Grand Canyon National Park

    The age of the Grand Canyon is a subject of ongoing scientific debate, but the most widely accepted estimate suggests that the Colorado River began carving the canyon about 6 million years ago. However, the exact timeline and processes involved in its formation continue to be a topic of research and discussion among geologists. The Grand Canyon's geology and its complex history make it a fascinating subject of study for scientists from around the world.

  • Cuyahoga Valley National Park

    Cuyahoga Valley National Park preserves a portion of the historic Ohio & Erie Canal, which played a crucial role in the economic development of Ohio and the Midwest in the 19th century. Visitors to the park can explore remnants of the canal, including locks and a canal towpath, providing a glimpse into the region's transportation history. This canal system once facilitated the movement of goods and people through the area and contributed to the growth of nearby communities.

  • Acadia National Park

    Acadia National Park features a popular hiking trail known as the Jordan Pond Path. What makes it unique is the tradition of serving popovers at the Jordan Pond House, which is located along the trail. Hikers often stop at the Jordan Pond House to enjoy these light, fluffy pastries with jam and tea while taking in scenic views of Jordan Pond and the surrounding mountains. It's a delightful and tasty tradition for visitors to the park.

  • Olympic National Park

    Olympic National Park is one of the few places in the United States where you can find a temperate rainforest. The Hoh Rainforest, located within the park, receives an average of 140 to 170 inches (3.5 to 4.3 meters) of rainfall annually. This lush, green environment is home to unique plant and animal species adapted to the high levels of precipitation, making it a fascinating and ecologically significant part of the park.
